Beneath the Surface

Understanding shark attacks in Australia — who, where, when, and why across 230 years of recorded incidents

FIT2179 · Data Visualisation 2

Australia records one of the highest rates of shark incidents in the world. Since 1791, 1,283 attacks have been documented — a record spanning over two centuries of ocean recreation, fishing, and coastal life. Yet the story is more nuanced than headlines suggest: most attacks are non-fatal, many are provoked, and the odds of being bitten remain extraordinarily low.

This visualisation explores the full dataset from the Australian Shark Incident Database (ASID), tracing patterns across geography, species, victim behaviour, time, and changing trends.

Where Do Attacks Happen?

Attacks by State

New South Wales and Queensland — Australia's most populous coastal states — record the highest total number of shark attacks. Together they account for over 65% of all incidents in the database.

Which States Are Most Deadly?

Western Australia stands out not just for its attack numbers but for its fatality proportion — historically the highest of any Australian state. NSW has the most attacks overall but a proportionally lower share of fatal outcomes, partly due to beach netting programs introduced in the 1930s.

Which Sharks Are Most Dangerous?

White sharks are responsible for the most attacks overall. Wobbegongs cause many incidents but are rarely fatal, while tiger sharks and bull sharks carry a disproportionately high fatality rate relative to their attack frequency.

🏄 Part Three

What Were Victims Doing?

Activity at time of attack and body part data reveals how behaviour shapes risk in the water.

What Were Victims Doing When Attacked?

Swimming is the most common activity at the time of attack, followed by boarding (surfing and bodyboarding). Spearfishing carries disproportionate risk — the presence of fish blood and erratic movement significantly elevates shark attention.

Lollipop chart: attacks by victim activity, sorted by frequency. JSON: vega_specs/07_activity.json

🦵

Which Body Parts Are Most at Risk?

Legs and feet are by far the most commonly bitten body parts. Torso injuries, while fewer in number, carry a far higher fatality rate — bites to the torso are more likely to sever major blood vessels.

📅 Part Four

When Do Attacks Occur?

Seasonal rhythms, time of day, and the long arc of history all shape when sharks bite.

Which Months and Times Are Most Dangerous?

Shark attacks peak in the Australian summer months of December through February. Late afternoon (3–6pm) sees the highest attack frequency — when the most people are in the water and shark visibility may be reduced by low sunlight angles.

How Have Attacks Changed Over Time?

Shark attack numbers have risen over the past century — but so has Australia's population and ocean recreation. The dual-axis chart shows that the per-capita rate has remained broadly stable even as absolute numbers rise.

Severity & Long-Term Trends

Has Survival Improved?

Despite rising absolute attack numbers, the fatality rate has declined significantly since the early 1900s. Improvements in emergency response, surf lifesaving, and beach surveillance have dramatically improved survival odds for attack victims.

🦈

Beneath the Surface — The Full Picture

Australia's shark attack story is one of risk in perspective. Yes, incidents have been recorded for over 230 years across every coastal state. But the data consistently shows that risk is shaped by behaviour, location, and season more than by random chance.

White sharks, tiger sharks, and bull sharks account for the majority of serious incidents. Summer months and coastal NSW and Queensland carry the highest exposure. Yet the fatality rate has more than halved since the mid-20th century — a testament to improved emergency response and beach safety infrastructure.

With 1,283 attacks over 234 years — fewer than six per year on average across an entire continent with one of the world's longest coastlines — the data confirms: the water is far safer than it feels.
